/// ROLE & FOCUS

I served as the Card Designer, Card Illustrator, and Game Mechanics Designer for this 4-6 player party-style board game. My work encompassed the complete card system: 10 unique item cards and 7 unique event cards, each requiring both visual illustration and mechanical design to support strategic gameplay.

/// CARD DESIGN

Each card needed to communicate its function instantly while maintaining the pirate-themed aesthetic. I organized cards into distinct categories: weapons (Knife, Cutlass, Blunderbuss, Pistol), utility items (Grappling Hook, Walk the Plank, Looking Glass), consumables (Bananas, Coconuts), and special cards (X Marks the Spot).

Figure 01. The card illustration.
/// MECHANICS DESIGN & BALANCE

I balanced card effects to create meaningful choices while preserving the party-game feel. Key mechanics included card combinations (Looking Glass + X Marks the Spot guarantees treasure), varying power levels, and usage conditions that create strategic trade-offs—forcing players to weigh immediate benefits against long-term positioning.

Early playtesting revealed that cards requiring discarding or combining were underutilized when drawn randomly from the board. I redesigned the distribution system to give each player 3 starting item cards, ensuring cards could be used as intended and dramatically improving strategic depth and player agency.

/// ITERATIVE REFINEMENT

Three rounds of playtesting drove key iterations: expanding event cards from 4 to 7 types to reduce repetition, redesigning the Storm card to target one player instead of multiple (creating targeted strategic consequences rather than blanket punishment), and doubling treasure tokens from 27 to 54 to prevent resource depletion.

The final system: 40 item cards (10 types × 4) and 21 event cards (7 types × 3), balanced for 30-60 minute sessions. This distribution provides clear probability expectations for strategic planning while maintaining variety across multiple playthroughs.

/// REFLECTION

This project demonstrated how visual design directly impacts gameplay comprehension. Balancing illustration work with mechanics design reinforced that form and function must inform each other: each card's visual identity needed to support its mechanical role while maintaining thematic coherence.

The iterative process revealed that small quantitative changes (starting hand size, card distribution) can dramatically shift player behavior and strategic depth. This experience strengthened my ability to use playtesting data to make informed design decisions that enhance both gameplay and player experience.
