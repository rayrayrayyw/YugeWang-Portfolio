TYPE Board Game

TOOLS Figma / Tabletop Simulator
YEAR 2024
LAUNCH PROJECT WEBSITE ↗

The Lost was selected for the SFU SIAT Project Gallery and featured on the SFU Showcase webpages.

/// ROLE & FOCUS

In this project, I served as the Gameplay Systems Designer, focusing on defining the core loop and tuning mechanics to support player decision-making.

My design approach centered on aligning gameplay structure with narrative intention—ensuring that mechanics, numbers, and emotional meaning worked together to create a coherent and impactful player experience.

/// SYSTEM MAPPING
Figure 01. Early Gameplay Structure & Spatial Strategy Sketches

The first image highlights early gameplay structure sketches, where I figured out how actions such as movement, ship navigation, damage tracking, and role responsibilities interact within the game board.

At this stage, I explored how spatial strategy and resource scarcity could drive player decision-making. The goal was to build the game as a systemic ecosystem, where tension and risk naturally emerged from how resources and damage were distributed across space.

/// MECHANICS & FRICTION
Figure 02. Role Interactions

The second image illustrates the development of system-level interactions. Each player takes on a unique crew role—Navigator, Carpenter, Captain, or Chef—managing different aspects of survival.

During testing, players reported that cooperation felt too effortless. To address this, I redesigned the interaction between morale, energy, and ship condition, and introduced a voting mechanic for high-risk events. This intentionally created friction, forcing negotiation and reinforcing interdependence among players.

Figure 03. Narrative Mechanics Integration

Connecting Mechanics to Emotion: A key design goal was to connect gameplay mechanics with the emotional arc of the narrative. The morale and resource systems were not only used to simulate survival difficulty but also reflected each character’s psychological resilience.

Repairing the ship symbolized rebuilding trust, and each key collected revealed a memory fragment. Progression became not just mechanical but emotional—players actively experienced tension, reliance, and reconciliation through the rules themselves.

/// TUNING & BALANCE
Figure 04. High-Fidelity Prototype & Component Balance

The final image shows the high-fidelity prototype, where the system was refined into a fully playable and thematically aligned experience. I balanced numerical values such as action cost, resource yield, and event probability to ensure sustained challenge while allowing multiple strategic paths.

The Morale Tracker, Ship Blueprint, and Memory Map were intentionally designed to make systemic consequences visible during play. As a result, roles felt distinct, decisions carried weight, and players consistently reported stronger emotional engagement linked directly to the game’s themes of trust and rediscovery.

/// REFLECTION

This project strengthened my ability to structure gameplay using systems thinking, merge mechanic-driven decisions with narrative impact, and improve experience quality through iterative playtesting and numerical tuning—core principles I continue to apply in my game design practice.
